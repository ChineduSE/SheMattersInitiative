Outreach 01  ·  Ijebu Igbo, Ogun State

Menstrual Hygiene Awareness

This was the very first outreach of She Matters Initiative — and it set the tone for everything that followed. We stepped into a community where many girls had never had an open, safe conversation about their menstrual health.

We spoke honestly and openly about the dangers of using clothes, foam, and other unsafe materials during their menstrual flow — practices that are far too common due to poverty and lack of access to sanitary products. We explained how these habits can lead to infections, health complications, and missed school days.

Girls were encouraged to ask questions, share their experiences, and leave with not just knowledge — but pads in their hands. Because awareness without action is incomplete.

Outreach 02  ·  Oru Ijebu, Ogun State

Educational Outreach

At Itamerin Junior and Senior Grammar School, Oru Ijebu, we showed up to celebrate excellence and plant seeds of ambition. Every girl in that school deserved to feel seen — and on this day, they were.

We awarded the best 3 students in each class — a deliberate act of recognition that told every girl watching: your hard work is noticed, and it matters. Excellence was put on display, not just by those who won, but by the school's entire culture that day.

Beyond the awards ceremony, we engaged the whole school in conversations about their futures, their rights, and the importance of staying in school. The energy in that compound was electric — hundreds of girls, all leaning in.

300+
Girls celebrated & empowered

Outreach 03  ·  Ikpoba Hill, Benin City, Edo State

She Leads 2025

She Leads 2025 was held at Army Secondary School, Ikpoba Hill, Benin City, Edo State — a project born from a simple but powerful question: what would happen if girls saw themselves as future leaders and politicians?

The major aim of this outreach was to awaken the love for leadership and politics in the hearts of girls. Through structured debates and open enlightenment conversations, girls were encouraged to speak up, take sides, defend their positions, and understand their rights as citizens of Nigeria.

Girls who had never raised their hands in class were standing up, speaking boldly, and surprising themselves. That's the She Leads effect — discovering the leader that was always already there.
